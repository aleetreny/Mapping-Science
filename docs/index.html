What This Website Shows

This website is a public visual companion to my Master's thesis. It lets you explore how scientific subfields look when their papers are represented as points in a shared scientific document space. Each point starts as a paper title and abstract. SPECTER2 converts that text into a 768-dimensional vector, and papers assigned to the same OpenAlex subfield form a document cloud.

How the Maps Are Made

  1. OpenAlex records are filtered to papers with usable titles and abstracts from 2000 to 2024.
  2. Each paper is embedded with SPECTER2, so it becomes a numerical point in the original 768-dimensional space.
  3. The thesis metrics are calculated from those high-dimensional document clouds: dispersion, local density, hubness, spectral structure, and centroid drift.
  4. The map shown here is then drawn by projecting a sample of papers into two dimensions with PCA, t-SNE, UMAP, or PHATE.

The important point is that the numbers and labels are not measured from the flat picture on the screen. They are computed before the 2D map is drawn, in the original 768-dimensional representation. The map is useful because it gives an intuitive view of clusters, gaps, and dense regions, but it necessarily loses information. For this reason, a subfield may have a high-dimensional metric value that does not look perfectly obvious in the 2D projection.

Reading note: the explorer works best by comparing a few related subfields instead of reading one map in isolation. Look for simple contrasts such as compact versus broad, dense versus loose, or stable versus moving. These differences are descriptive, so they should be read as patterns in the literature, not as rankings of better or worse disciplines.
