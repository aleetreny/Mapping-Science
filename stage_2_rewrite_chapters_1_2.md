## Objective

The new text must be:

- direct;
- problem-led;
- technically precise;
- compact;
- natural in rhythm;
- methodologically explicit;
- free of repeated claims and formulaic rhetorical patterns;
- consistent with the academic register identified in the three reference papers.

Do not imitate or copy distinctive wording from those papers.

Keep approximately the current combined length of Chapters 1 and 2, preferably slightly shorter. Do not expand the chapters merely to sound more academic.

## Chapter 1: Introduction

The introduction must establish, in a clear order:

1. the concrete problem:
   existing labels, citation maps, topic models, and projected maps do not directly measure how papers are structurally arranged inside a field;

2. the analytical object:
   scientific fields are treated as distributions of scientific-document embeddings;

3. the contribution:
   an eight-metric structural morphology framework, temporal recomputation of the same metrics, and a separate centroid-drift diagnostic;

4. the three research questions;

5. the descriptive, non-causal scope;

6. a short roadmap.

### Specific corrections

- Remove repeated formulations of:
  - morphology is not taxonomy;
  - morphology is not semantic location;
  - morphology is not visualisation;
  - the thesis does not replace other methods.
- State the full conceptual distinction once, clearly, rather than in several variations.
- Avoid repeated “not X, but Y” constructions.
- Avoid phrases that merely announce the contribution instead of showing it.
- Make the student’s work visible through precise first-person statements where useful.
- Keep the roadmap brief and functional.
- Preserve the three current research questions unless a very minor wording adjustment is necessary for grammar or flow. Do not change their substantive meaning.

## Chapter 2: Background and Related Work

The chapter must explain:

1. what classical science mapping measures;
2. what scientific-document embeddings add;
3. why semantic location alone is insufficient;
4. how dispersion, local density, hubness, and spectral structure define the missing analytical object;
5. how this thesis differs from, but complements, taxonomies, citation-based maps, topic models, and dimensionality-reduced visualisations.

### Specific corrections

- Make the literature review problem-led rather than source-by-source.
- Use references to support analytical decisions, not as an inventory.
- Avoid repeating the same contrast already established in Chapter 1.
- Sharpen the gap:
  existing methods describe relations, topics, labels, or positions; the thesis measures the internal distributional shape of field-level document clouds in a fixed representation space.
- Preserve relevant citations and attribution.
- Do not introduce new literature unless a clear claim in these two chapters requires support and the reference is verified.
- Do not move detailed methodological justifications from Chapter 3 into Chapter 2.

## Style requirements

Apply the Stage 1 writing standard:

- one clear purpose per paragraph;
- claim or decision, followed by evidence or definition, followed by consequence;
- varied sentence length and paragraph rhythm;
- no generic filler;
- no repeated section-ending summaries;
- no formulaic figure-discussion pattern;
- limitations stated once in the most relevant place;
- technical transitions rather than theatrical signposting;
- restrained use of first person;
- no exaggerated claims of novelty.

Avoid repeated constructions such as:

- “This thesis does not…”
- “This matters because…”
- “The result is deliberately modest…”
- “X is useful, but incomplete…”
- “Not X, but Y…”

They may be used once if genuinely natural, but not as recurring templates.
