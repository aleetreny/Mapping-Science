\section{Following Shape Through Time}

Temporal morphology is measured through five fixed windows: 2000--2004, 2005--2009, 2010--2014, 2015--2019, and 2020--2024. Within each window, the eight non-temporal structural metrics from the reduced core are recomputed in the original SPECTER2 embedding space. Centroid drift, radial expansion slope, and recent novelty remain full-period indicators because they already summarize movement over time.

For comparability, the windowed plots use metric-level z-scores computed across all completed subfield-window observations. These values are relative positions within the observed temporal distribution, not inferential statistics. The temporal stage is nearly complete: 1,204 of 1,205 expected subfield-window rows were computed. The only unavailable row is Computational Mathematics in 2000--2004, which had fewer than the minimum number of papers for that window and is excluded from first--last subfield change rankings.

\section{Domain-Level Temporal Trajectories}

The domain view gives the broad orientation. Across all subfields, mean median centroid distance declines from 0.0858 in 2000--2004 to 0.0750 in 2020--2024, mean median kNN distance declines from 0.0923 to 0.0756, and PCA D80 falls from 81.3 to 71.1. At the same time, kNN distance variability rises. The large-scale pattern is therefore not simple contraction; it is movement toward closer neighborhoods with more uneven local distance structure.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\caption[Domain-level temporal trajectories]{Domain-level temporal trajectories across five-year windows. Values are mean standardized subfield-window metrics within each domain.}
\label{fig:domain_temporal_trajectories}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_07_domain_temporal_trajectories.png}
\end{figure}

Physical Sciences remain the most dispersed domain for most of the period and retain the highest average spectral dimensionality. Health Sciences move furthest downward in local distance and PCA D80, ending with the tightest local neighborhoods and the lowest average dimensionality. Life Sciences follow the same direction but with a clearer rise in kNN distance variability. Social Sciences are less extreme in the domain averages. The value of the figure is that it makes movement visible; static profiles alone would hide these different paths.

The largest paths are not concentrated in a single disciplinary neighborhood. Human Factors and Ergonomics is spatially distant from the high-movement computational subfields, while Infectious Diseases and General Dentistry occupy a separate upper-left to central band. Large movement also has different paths: Modeling and Simulation and Ecological Modeling show long directional trajectories; Energy Engineering and Power Technology moves strongly but stays in a lower region; the computer-science paths cluster closer together. The key point is that centroid movement and morphology change are related but not interchangeable.

Field summaries add another layer. Figure~\ref{fig:field_temporal_delta_heatmap} shows that Pharmacology, Toxicology and Pharmaceutics, Dentistry, Chemical Engineering, Medicine, and Energy have especially strong declines in median centroid distance. Dentistry, Energy, Immunology and Microbiology, Medicine, and Chemistry show large declines in median kNN distance. These are related but not interchangeable changes.

\begin{figure}[H]

\label{fig:field_temporal_delta_heatmap}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth,height=0.80\textheight,keepaspectratio]{figures/fig_07_field_temporal_delta_heatmap.png}

Hubness is the clearest departure from a simple contraction story. Energy and Immunology and Microbiology move upward in hub concentration, while Computer Science, Engineering, Psychology, and Health Professions move downward. Computer Science also has one of the largest drops in PCA D80, but its hubness trajectory differs from fields that become more locally centralized.

The full-period temporal indicators summarize movement relative to earlier configurations. Figure~\ref{fig:full_period_temporal_indicators_by_domain} shows that every domain contains both ordinary and extreme movement. Social Sciences contain the strongest centroid-drift outlier, Human Factors and Ergonomics. Physical Sciences contain many high-novelty and positive radial-expansion subfields, including Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition and Signal Processing. Health Sciences are lower on recent novelty as a domain, but several medical subfields still appear among the most dynamic windowed profiles.

\section{The Most Dynamic Scientific Regions}

The strongest first--last profile changes are concentrated in a small set of subfields. Philosophy has the largest windowed structural displacement, followed by General Dentistry, Molecular Medicine, General Psychology, Infectious Diseases, Renewable Energy, Sustainability and the Environment, Immunology and Allergy, Virology, Oncology, and Signal Processing. The ranking measures Euclidean distance between the initial and final standardized profiles across the eight windowed structural metrics.

The drivers behind these cases are deliberately mixed. Philosophy is dominated by a sharp decrease in centroid IQR, while Molecular Medicine moves in the opposite direction on that same metric. General Dentistry is driven by a decline in PCA spectral entropy. General Psychology changes primarily through reduced kNN distance variability, whereas Immunology and Allergy and Virology change through increases in that metric. The ranking therefore identifies strong temporal movement, not one common pattern.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\caption[Contrasting subfield trajectory profiles]{Contrasting subfield trajectory profiles. Each panel tracks four standardized windowed metrics for a dynamic subfield.}
\label{fig:selected_subfield_trajectories}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_07_selected_subfield_trajectories.png}

The trajectory panels make those differences concrete. Philosophy changes sharply after the first window and then stabilizes. Molecular Medicine becomes locally denser while remaining high in kNN distance variability. General Dentistry becomes more locally compact and spectrally simpler, but its distance variability rises late. Signal Processing moves differently again: dispersion and kNN CV rise while PCA D80 falls. These cases show why temporal morphology cannot be reduced to one trend line.

Temporal morphology describes how embedded structure changes; it does not explain why the changes occurred. Five-year windows reduce annual noise and keep sample sizes usable, but they can hide shorter shocks or transitions. The comparison matters because morphology changes unevenly across the 2000--2024 subfield population, with the strongest movement concentrated in particular scientific regions rather than distributed evenly across the hierarchy.

\paragraph{What this result means.}
Scientific morphology changes over time, but not in one universal direction. Some fields become locally denser, some become more uneven, some move far from their earlier semantic center, and others remain comparatively stable. Temporal movement reveals structure that a full-period static profile hides.
