\section{From Similarity to Exploratory Typologies}

The previous chapters showed the same empirical object from three angles: static profile variation, temporal movement, and pairwise morphological similarity. This chapter compresses those relations into a small set of exploratory typologies. The goal is not to discover natural categories of science. Clustering is used here as a descriptive device: it groups subfields whose eleven-metric morphology is similar enough to make repeated profile patterns easier to name, compare, and question.

The unit clustered is the 241 eligible OpenAlex subfields. Each subfield is represented by the reduced eleven-metric core from Chapter~5: dispersion around the centroid, local density and hubness, spectral structure, and the three full-period temporal indicators. These metrics were computed from the original SPECTER2 embedding space; no paper-level clusters, taxonomy labels, UMAP coordinates, or PCA coordinates enter the clustering. Projections appear below only as visual summaries of the resulting metric-profile space.

Before clustering, each metric is centered by the cross-subfield median and scaled by the interquartile range. Robust scaling is used because several metrics have visible tails and because the typology should not be driven by a small number of extreme subfields. Domain and field labels are retained only as metadata for interpretation. They are not used as features, targets, or constraints.

\section{Exploring Candidate Typology Models}

The exploration compared hierarchical clustering, \(k\)-means, Gaussian mixture models, and distance variants across \(k=3,\ldots,10\). The main hierarchical family used Ward linkage on Euclidean distances in standardized profile space, with both robust and z-score scaling. Average linkage was tested with Euclidean and correlation distances. \(k\)-means was included as a centroid-based comparison; Gaussian mixtures were used only diagnostically because their covariance assumptions are harder to defend in this small, eleven-dimensional profile space. Density-based clustering was not retained in the final comparison because it could not be incorporated as a reproducible component of the active thesis pipeline.

Figure~\ref{fig:typology_quality_by_k} summarizes the diagnostic result. Separation is modest. The selected Ward solution at \(k=5\) has a silhouette score of \(0.133\), which is not evidence of strong natural clusters. Some alternatives obtain higher silhouette scores, but for reasons that make them less useful as typologies. Average-link Euclidean clustering, for example, reaches \(0.361\) at \(k=5\) by producing one cluster of 230 subfields plus tiny fragments. Correlation linkage separates profile shapes more sharply, but it downweights absolute level differences that are substantively important here: compactness, dispersion, hubness, and novelty are not just relative patterns across metrics.

The selected solution is therefore conservative rather than optimal in a single-score sense. It avoids singleton clusters at \(k=5\), whereas the same robust Ward family begins peeling off singletons at higher \(k\). It also gives interpretable profile contrasts without collapsing the space into one giant group. The stability checks qualify the result. Z-score Ward agrees only moderately with the selected solution (ARI \(=0.306\), AMI \(=0.409\)); excluding the three temporal metrics gives a related but different static-only solution (ARI \(=0.372\), AMI \(=0.405\)); and family-balanced robust Ward agrees more strongly (ARI \(=0.548\), AMI \(=0.561\)) but creates a singleton. Across 100 random 80 percent subsamples, mean ARI is \(0.355\) and mean AMI is \(0.444\). These values support a weak typology: useful anchors, fuzzy boundaries.

\section{Selected Morphological Typology}

The final typology uses Ward hierarchical clustering with \(k=5\) on robust-scaled eleven-metric subfield profiles. The five groups are named by their mean standardized profiles rather than by domain composition or prestige. Table~\ref{tab:typology_summary} gives the compact version, and Figure~\ref{fig:typology_profile_heatmap} shows the full profile signature.

The largest type, T1 Broad sparse profiles, contains 89 subfields. Its signature is high median kNN distance, high centroid distance, and relatively low local variability and hub concentration. It is a broad-territory profile: subfields in this group tend to occupy more extended semantic regions and have farther nearest neighbors, but their local neighborhoods are not especially uneven.

T2 Compact low-dispersion profiles contains 49 subfields. It has low centroid dispersion and low median kNN distance, while retaining comparatively high PCA dimensionality and hub Gini. This is not simply a low-complexity group. It is compact in distance terms, but its variation still requires several spectral directions and may concentrate around local hubs.

T3 Low-dimensional locally uneven profiles contains 67 subfields. Its profile is below average on PCA dimensionality and spectral entropy, while kNN distance variability and centroid drift are above average. These subfields are not the broadest regions in the map, but their local neighborhoods are uneven and their full-period positions have moved more than the median subfield.

T4 Temporal novelty profiles contains 26 subfields and is the most temporally distinctive type. It has very high centroid drift and recent novelty, positive radial slope, and low spectral entropy. The label does not mean that these subfields are intellectually new in a sociological sense. It means their recent papers are farther from the early semantic core and their centroids move strongly under the full-period SPECTER2 representation.

T5 Uneven dispersion outliers is small, with 10 subfields. It is defined by very high centroid IQR and kNN distance variability, plus elevated hub Gini and low spectral entropy. This type is best read as an outlier profile family rather than a stable large class: its members share an unusual combination of internal spread and local unevenness.

\section{Typologies Across Domains and Border Cases}

The typologies partly align with OpenAlex domains, but none is a pure domain proxy. Figure~\ref{fig:typology_domain_composition} makes this visible. T1 and T4 are dominated by Physical Sciences, with 51 of 89 and 17 of 26 subfields respectively, but both include subfields from all four domains. T2 is split mainly between Social Sciences and Health Sciences, with Physical Sciences still present. T3 is strongest in Health Sciences and Social Sciences but also includes Life Sciences. T5 is evenly divided between Life Sciences and Health Sciences at the top, with one Social Sciences and one Physical Sciences member.

The border cases are as important as the prototypes. Table~\ref{tab:typology_examples} lists the closest subfields to each typology centroid, the smallest-margin cases, and internally distant members. Because Ward clustering does not assign points by nearest centroid, a few border cases sit closer to an adjacent typology centroid than to their own. That is not a failure of the method; it is a useful warning that the partition is a hierarchical cut through continuous profile space.

The examples also prevent the labels from becoming too smooth. Human Factors and Ergonomics is a distant member of the temporal novelty type rather than a generic representative. Philosophy sits inside the uneven-dispersion outlier group but is not interchangeable with Toxicology or Fluid Flow and Transfer Processes. These cases are reminders that the typology compresses profile relations; it does not replace individual subfield interpretation.

\section{Interpretive Limits of Clustering}

The clustering evidence is weak in the precise sense that the profile space does not break into clean, separated groups. This matters. The typologies should not be read as objective disciplinary boundaries, natural kinds, quality rankings, causal mechanisms, or replacements for the OpenAlex hierarchy. They are labels for recurrent combinations of dispersion, local density, hubness, spectral structure, and temporal movement.

No single clustering criterion can settle this interpretation problem. Different reasonable algorithms reward different geometry: Ward favors compact Euclidean groups, correlation linkage favors profile shape, \(k\)-means favors centroid separation, and mixture models favor distributional fit. That is why the chapter uses diagnostics and sensitivity checks rather than treating a single score as decisive \parencite{kleinberg2002clustering}. The selected solution is defensible because it is interpretable, avoids obvious singleton artifacts at \(k=5\), retains the temporal metrics while surviving a static-only comparison, and helps synthesize the empirical chapters. It is not strong evidence that science has five morphological types.

The useful conclusion is more modest. Scientific subfields occupy a continuous morphology space, but that space has repeated profile patterns. Some subfields are broad and sparse, some compact, some locally uneven, some temporally novel, and a small set unusually dispersed or irregular. Those patterns cut across domains while still carrying domain tendencies. Chapter~9 therefore closes the empirical arc by turning many pairwise and metric-level observations into a cautious typological vocabulary: not a taxonomy of science, but a map legend for reading morphology.

Appendix~C repeats the clustering exercise using subfield centroid embeddings rather than morphology profiles. The centroid solution separates somewhat more clearly and aligns more strongly with OpenAlex domains, but its low agreement with the morphological typology confirms that Chapter~9 is describing shape profiles rather than topic-location clusters. Appendix~C also tests temporal-trajectory and hybrid centroid--morphology clustering. Temporal slopes form a clearer dynamic sensitivity partition, while hybrid clustering is stable but strongly domain-like when centroid information is weighted heavily. These checks clarify the boundary of the result: dynamic change and semantic location can be summarized separately, but neither justifies replacing the main weak/descriptive morphology typology.
