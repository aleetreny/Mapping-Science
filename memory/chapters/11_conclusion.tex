% Chapter 11: Conclusion

This thesis measured the morphology of scientific fields in a dense scientific embedding space. It did not try to discover the true map of science or replace existing taxonomies. Its goal was more specific: to ask how OpenAlex subfields, represented by SPECTER2-embedded title--abstract records, occupy semantic space as distributions of papers.

The empirical pipeline built a balanced OpenAlex corpus covering 2000--2024, linked each retained work to a row-aligned SPECTER2 vector, and measured subfields through an eleven-metric morphology core. The metrics summarized dispersion, local density, hubness, spectral structure, and temporal movement in the original SPECTER2 space. Low-dimensional projections were used only for display.

Four findings carry the thesis. First, broad domains have morphological tendencies, but they do not determine morphology. Physical Sciences are generally more dispersed and spectrally extended; Health Sciences are more locally dense and hub-concentrated; field and subfield variation inside domains remains large. Second, scientific morphology changes over time in structured but heterogeneous ways: several metrics move toward closer neighborhoods and lower spectral dimensionality, while local unevenness and hubness do not follow one universal pattern. Third, morphological similarity cuts across taxonomy, and temporal convergence is selective rather than global. Field-level profile space becomes more separated on average between 2000--2004 and 2020--2024, even as some field pairs move substantially closer. Fourth, static morphology is weakly clustered, while temporal trajectories form a clearer and more stable dynamic typology. Semantic location aligns more strongly with OpenAlex domains than morphology does.

The central result is not a new taxonomy of science, but a way to measure why no single taxonomy is enough. Scientific fields have positions, shapes, trajectories, and relationships. A domain label captures only part of that organization. A centroid captures location but not dispersion. A static profile captures shape but not change. A trajectory captures movement but not necessarily topic. A cluster can summarize a region of the space, but it should not be mistaken for a natural kind.

Embedding-space morphology makes scientific structure measurable without forcing it into one visual or categorical form. Science can be read as a landscape of distributions: some broad and sparse, some compact and dense, some hub-concentrated, some spectrally extended, some moving sharply through time. Those patterns cross taxonomic boundaries while still carrying domain tendencies.

The final claim is careful, but not small. Under a transparent corpus, representation model, and metric framework, treating static shape, temporal change, taxonomy, and semantic location as separate dimensions makes the structure of science more measurable, and also more resistant to simplistic maps.
