% Chapter 11: Conclusion

This thesis measured scientific fields as distributions of papers in a dense scientific embedding space. It asked what can be learned when OpenAlex subfields, represented by SPECTER2-embedded title--abstract records, are measured by the shape and movement of their embedded paper distributions.

The empirical pipeline built a balanced OpenAlex corpus covering 2000--2024, linked each retained work to a row-aligned SPECTER2 vector, and measured 241 analysis subfields through an eleven-metric morphology core. The metrics summarized dispersion, local density, hubness, spectral structure, and temporal movement in the original 768-dimensional SPECTER2 space. Low-dimensional projections were used only for display.

Four findings carry the thesis. First, broad domains have morphological tendencies, but they do not determine field shape. Physical Sciences are generally more dispersed and spectrally extended; Health Sciences are more locally dense and hub-concentrated; field and subfield variation inside domains remains large. Second, scientific morphology changes over time in structured but heterogeneous ways. Several metrics move toward closer neighborhoods and lower spectral dimensionality, while local unevenness and hubness do not follow one universal pattern. Third, morphological similarity cuts across taxonomy, and temporal convergence is selective rather than global. Field-level profile space becomes more separated on average between 2000--2004 and 2020--2024, even as some field pairs move substantially closer. Fourth, static morphology is weakly clustered, with Ward \(k=5\) silhouette \(=0.133\), while temporal trajectories form a clearer and more stable dynamic typology, with silhouette \(=0.396\) and mean subsample ARI \(=0.725\). This does not make the dynamic groups natural kinds; it shows that fields are more clearly summarized by how they change than by their full-period morphology. Semantic location aligns more strongly with OpenAlex domains than morphology does.

The central result is this separation. A field has a label, a semantic location, a shape, a trajectory, and relations to other fields. A domain label captures only part of that organization. A centroid captures location but not dispersion. A static profile captures shape but not change. A trajectory captures movement but not necessarily topic. A cluster summarizes a region of a space as an interpretive profile, not as a natural kind.

The thesis therefore argues something limited and useful: once papers are represented in a common scientific-document space, fields can be measured as distributions. That makes it possible to separate what a field is called, where it is located, how it is shaped, how it changes, and which other fields it resembles. This separation is the thesis's main contribution.

A scientific field is not only a name. It is also a distribution of papers, with a center, a radius, local neighborhoods, hubs, directions of variation, and a history of movement. Measuring these features gives expert interpretation a clearer object to examine.
