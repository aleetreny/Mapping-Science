\section{Reading Shape in One Cross-Section}

Static comparison gives each subfield one full-period morphology profile using the eleven metrics defined in Chapter~5. Temporal indicators are included as full-period movement summaries, but trajectories are not modeled window by window until Chapter~7.

The analysis uses the OpenAlex hierarchy at three levels: 241 eligible subfields, 26 fields, and 4 domains. Subfield profiles are the direct unit of measurement; field and domain profiles are averages over constituent subfields. For visual comparison, each metric is standardized within the level being plotted. A red cell or point means above-average values for that metric at that level, and a blue one means below-average values. The colors compare morphology across metrics; they do not change the raw metric definitions.

Size is not morphology, dispersion is not local density, and static shape is not causality. Those distinctions frame the chapter; the empirical question is how far the OpenAlex hierarchy aligns with measured field shape.

\section{Domain-Level Morphological Patterns}

At the broadest scale, the four domains differ, but not in a cleanly taxonomic way. Physical Sciences stand out as the most dispersed domain profile: median centroid distance, 90th-percentile centroid distance, median kNN distance, PCA dimensionality, centroid drift, and recent novelty are all above the domain average. Because domain-level z-scores are computed across only four domains, they are used as relative visual contrasts rather than inferential statistics.

\begin{figure}[H]

\label{fig:domain_metric_profile_heatmap}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_06_domain_metric_profile_heatmap.png}
\end{figure}

Health Sciences show the opposite pattern on several dimensions. They have the lowest median kNN distance, indicating tight local neighborhoods, and the highest kNN in-degree Gini, indicating stronger concentration around neighborhood hubs. Their PCA dimensionality and spectral entropy are below the domain average. Under this representation, the health profile is therefore locally dense and more hub-dominated, not simply compact.

Social Sciences are comparatively compact at the domain level, with lower centroid dispersion and lower hub concentration. Life Sciences sit closer to the center of most static metrics but show stronger negative recent novelty and radial contraction summaries. These domain averages are useful first signals, but they are blunt instruments. This is important because the figure shows domain tendencies, while the rest of the chapter shows why those tendencies are not enough.

\section{Field and Subfield Variation}

Field-level profiles show why domain summaries are insufficient. Physical Sciences contain highly dispersed fields such as Physics and Astronomy, Computer Science, Engineering, and Environmental Science, but also more compact fields such as Energy and Mathematics. Health Sciences include dense fields such as Dentistry and Nursing, while Medicine sits closer to the center of the field distribution. Broad domains have tendencies, not uniform morphologies.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\caption[Field-level morphological profiles]{Field-level standardized morphological profiles. Rows are fields; the colored strip marks the parent domain.}
\label{fig:field_metric_profile_heatmap}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth,height=0.78\textheight,keepaspectratio]{figures/fig_06_field_metric_profile_heatmap.png}
\end{figure}

The most visible contrast is local structure. Chemistry, Physics and Astronomy, Materials Science, Engineering, and Environmental Science have high median kNN distances, meaning that even near neighbors tend to be farther away. Dentistry, Nursing, Immunology and Microbiology, Veterinary, and Health Professions have lower median kNN distances. This does not mean that the latter fields are narrower in every sense; their papers are packed into closer local neighborhoods under the SPECTER2 embedding space. The field-level view matters because it breaks the simple domain story without abandoning the domain comparison.

At subfield level, the boxplots in Figure~\ref{fig:subfield_metric_distributions_by_domain} show wide distributions and outliers around the domain medians. Physical Sciences have the highest median subfield dispersion and kNN distance, but some physical subfields are compact. Health Sciences have lower local distances on average, but hubness varies widely. Social Sciences contain both compact applied areas and highly atypical humanities subfields.

\section{Morphological Extremes and Profile Space}

The clearest evidence comes from the extremes. Nuclear and High Energy Physics, Artificial Intelligence, and Electrical and Electronic Engineering have the highest median centroid distances, indicating unusually broad semantic territories. Applied Microbiology and Biotechnology, General Energy, and Research and Theory sit at the compact end of the same metric. For local density, Molecular Biology and Materials Chemistry have sparse nearest-neighbor structure, whereas Applied Microbiology and Biotechnology and Internal Medicine are among the tightest local neighborhoods.

\input{tables/tab_06_extreme_subfields}

Hubness cuts across those patterns. General Materials Science, Electrochemistry, and Internal Medicine have high kNN in-degree Gini values, meaning their local neighborhoods concentrate around a smaller set of papers. Artificial Intelligence and Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition, despite being highly dispersed and temporally novel, sit at the low-hubness end. A subfield can be broad without being hub-dominated, and a compact subfield can still be organized around strong local hubs.

The exploratory profile map in Figure~\ref{fig:metric_profile_pca_map} summarizes the eleven standardized metrics into two principal components. It is a visualization of metric profiles, not paper-level semantic space. The first two axes explain 30.9 percent and 28.3 percent of the standardized profile variance. Domains overlap in the middle, while specific subfields form the most informative extremes. That overlap reinforces the main argument: morphology is related to taxonomy, but not reducible to it.

For readers who want to inspect projected paper-level shapes behind selected cases, Appendix~\ref{app:umap-atlas} provides illustrative UMAP density maps. Those maps are kept in the appendix because they are visual diagnostics, not the coordinate system from which the metrics are computed.

Static profiles are strongest for comparison and weakest for explanation. They show that Physical Sciences are generally more dispersed and spectrally extended, Health Sciences are more locally dense and hub-concentrated, and field-level variation prevents simple domain stories. High dispersion, low kNN distance, or high recent novelty are structural descriptions, not rankings of quality, maturity, simplicity, or causal change.

\paragraph{What this result means.}
Broad domains have tendencies, but they do not determine morphology. Physical Sciences are often more dispersed, and Health Sciences are often more locally dense, but variation inside each domain is large. This matters because the shape of a field cannot be inferred from its domain label alone.
