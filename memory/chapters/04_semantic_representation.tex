\section{Title--Abstract Embeddings}

Each retained work is represented by the concatenation of its title and abstract. Titles usually identify the central object, method, or contribution of a paper, while abstracts summarize the research problem, evidence, and conclusions in a standardized form. This input is less complete than full text, but it is far more consistently available at corpus scale and more semantically informative than bibliographic metadata alone.

The synchronized full corpus contains 2,378,036 text-eligible works across 252 OpenAlex subfields, 26 fields, and 4 domains. The embedding analysis uses 2,344,927 embedded works across 241 analysis subfields because downstream metrics require valid vectors, aligned metadata, and sufficient observations per analytical unit.

The representation step maps each title--abstract record to a dense vector:

\[
f_{\theta}(x_i) = z_i \in \mathbb{R}^{768},
\]

where \(x_i\) is the title--abstract text of work \(i\), \(f_{\theta}\) is the trained embedding model, and \(z_i\) is the resulting vector. The vector is an operational representation of a paper under the model, not a direct measurement of scientific meaning.

\section{SPECTER2 and the Analysis Matrix}

The active model is SPECTER2, evaluated in the SciRepEval framework for scientific document representations \parencite{singh2023scirepeval}. It is appropriate because the thesis compares sets of papers as scientific works, rather than estimating word frequencies, topic shares, or sentence-level paraphrase similarity. Its 768-dimensional output gives every retained work a common numerical form.

The analysis matrix is

\[
Z = (z_1, \ldots, z_n)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times 768},
\qquad n = 2{,}344{,}927.
\]

Each row corresponds to one embedded work and remains aligned with its OpenAlex metadata: work identifier, publication year, subfield, field, and domain. This row alignment is the contract between corpus construction and morphology measurement. If row \(i\) contains \(z_i\), the metadata row \(i\) must describe the same work. All later grouping by subfield, domain, or time window depends on this invariant.

\section{Measurement and Display}

All quantitative morphology metrics are computed in the original SPECTER2 space, with 768 dimensions. Distances, neighborhoods, densities, centroids, dispersion statistics, and temporal movements are measured before any transformation for visualization. An embedding-space metric therefore refers to a metric computed from the original vectors unless explicitly stated otherwise.

This choice avoids adding a second geometry between the representation model and the metric definitions. The original space is still learned, high-dimensional, and shaped by SPECTER2's training objective, but it is the space in which the model represents documents. The thesis treats it as a common analytical geometry for comparison, not as an objective map of scientific knowledge.

Projected displays are used only for visual inspection and communication. UMAP preserves selected aspects of local structure under its own assumptions \parencite{mcinnes2018umap}; apparent separation, overlap, or cluster shape may depend on hyperparameters, sampling, density variation, and the projection objective. Similar cautions apply to nonlinear embeddings more generally \parencite{chari2023specious,wattenberg2016tsne}.

\section{Limits for Morphological Analysis}

The representation depends on title and abstract availability, so fields with different abstracting conventions may be represented unevenly. SPECTER2 inherits biases from its training data, scientific-language coverage, and citation-related supervision. The 768-dimensional vector compresses a paper and cannot retain every theoretical, methodological, or evidential feature. High-dimensional distance calculations can also be affected by anisotropy, concentration, and uneven local density.

These limits constrain interpretation without invalidating the analysis. The output is a row-aligned SPECTER2 matrix linked to validated OpenAlex metadata. It makes it possible to treat each subfield as a distribution of papers in a shared semantic space and to compare those distributions using common geometric operations.
