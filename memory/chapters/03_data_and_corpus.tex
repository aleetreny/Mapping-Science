\section{OpenAlex as Analytical Frame}

OpenAlex is the primary bibliographic data source for the thesis: an open index of scholarly works, authors, venues, institutions, and research concepts designed for large-scale reproducible analysis \parencite{priem2022openalex}. Its openness is methodologically important because the corpus can be documented, audited, and reconstructed without proprietary infrastructure.

The main analytical unit is the OpenAlex \textit{subfield}. Subfields are more granular than domains while remaining stable enough for comparative and temporal analysis. They are aggregated into \textit{fields} and \textit{domains} when broader summaries are required. Each work is assigned to a subfield through its primary-topic subfield, preserving a one-work--one-subfield structure for the main analysis.

This taxonomy is used pragmatically. OpenAlex subfields are transparent reference units, not definitive boundaries of scientific knowledge. Coverage differences, metadata incompleteness, classification uncertainty, and language bias remain part of the empirical object. Comparative evidence shows that open databases can support broad science mapping while varying by database, discipline, and document type \parencite{martinmartin2021google}; recent work also documents OpenAlex metadata limits, including abstract coverage, despite broadly comparable reference coverage under cleaned comparisons with Web of Science and Scopus \parencite{culbert2025reference}. The corpus is therefore a controlled analytical sample, not a census of global scientific production.

\section{Sampling a Comparable Corpus}

The empirical analyses use a synchronized OpenAlex title--abstract corpus covering publications from 2000 to 2024. The full corpus contains 2,378,036 text-eligible works across 252 subfields, 26 fields, and 4 domains. The embedding-based analysis uses a row-aligned subset of 2,344,927 embedded works across 241 analysis subfields.

The corpus uses a balanced annual sampling design. For subfield \(s\) and year \(t\), the planned annual sample size is

\[
n_{s,t}^{planned} = \min \left( N_{s,t}^{eligible}, 400 \right),
\]

where \(N_{s,t}^{eligible}\) is the number of works satisfying the eligibility filters in that subfield-year cell. Across the 25-year period, the maximum full-period target is \(400 \times 25 = 10{,}000\) works per subfield. The complete plan contains 2,431,303 planned works. The synchronized corpus retains 2,378,036 works, leaving a total shortfall of 53,267 works.

Capacity from sparse cells is not redistributed to denser cells. When a subfield-year cell contains fewer than 400 eligible works, the missing observations are recorded as shortfall. This preserves temporal structure and prevents large or recent fields from dominating later measures only because they produce more eligible records.

\section{Validation and Coverage}

The synchronized corpus covers all four OpenAlex domains. Physical Sciences contributes the largest number of retained works, followed by Health Sciences, Social Sciences, and Life Sciences.

\input{tables/tab_03_domain_distribution}

The domain distribution reflects the OpenAlex taxonomy, the annual cap, and eligible title--abstract availability. It should not be read as the true distribution of global scientific output. The sampling design deliberately reshapes observed publication volume to support comparable semantic measurements across subfields and years.

Validation checks the planned design and the required metadata and text conditions. The synchronized validation report did not fail. Critical checks are clean: no duplicate work identifiers, no rows outside 2000--2024, no rows in 2025 or 2026, no missing subfield, field, domain, or primary-topic identifiers, no missing title or abstract fields, no records below the minimum text thresholds, no non-English rows, and no disallowed work types.

The remaining limitation is coverage rather than validity. Out of 6,300 diagnostic subfield-year rows, 5,493 reach the annual target of 400 works and 807 remain below target. These below-target cells account for the total shortfall of 53,267 works.

\section{From Corpus to Analysis}

Corpus construction proceeds through taxonomy definition, annual count estimation, corpus planning, sample construction, work retrieval, eligibility filtering, and validation. The resulting title--abstract corpus is the textual basis for the embedding stage. Appendix A records artifact names and execution commands.

The embedding stage converts the validated corpus into a row-aligned analysis matrix. The analysis subset is smaller than the full corpus because metric computation requires valid embeddings, unambiguous metadata alignment, and sufficient observations per analytical unit. It contains 2,344,927 embedded works across 241 analysis subfields.

The later results are measurements of a balanced OpenAlex title--abstract corpus represented in a shared embedding space. They support comparative claims about retained scientific works under a specific data source, taxonomy, sampling design, and text representation, not claims about total production, impact, funding, or causal scientific development.
