\section{Data Source and Analytical Unit}

This thesis uses OpenAlex as its primary bibliographic data source. OpenAlex is a fully open index of scholarly works, authors, venues, institutions, and research concepts, designed to support large-scale and reproducible analyses of scientific literature \parencite{priem2022openalex}. Its openness is methodologically important: the corpus can be documented, audited, and reconstructed without dependence on proprietary bibliographic infrastructures.

The main analytical unit is the OpenAlex \textit{subfield}. Subfields provide a level of resolution more granular than broad domains while remaining stable enough for comparative and temporal analysis. They are aggregated into \textit{fields} and \textit{domains} when broader summaries are required. Each work is assigned to a subfield through its primary-topic subfield, preserving a one-work--one-subfield structure for the main analysis.

This taxonomy is used pragmatically. OpenAlex subfields are not treated as natural or definitive boundaries of scientific knowledge, but as a transparent reference system for comparing semantic organization across scientific regions. As with all large bibliographic databases, OpenAlex is affected by coverage differences, metadata incompleteness, classification uncertainty, and language bias. Comparative evidence shows that open databases can support broad science mapping while still varying by database, discipline, and document type \parencite{martinmartin2021google}; recent work also documents OpenAlex metadata limitations, including abstract coverage, despite broadly comparable reference coverage under cleaned comparisons with Web of Science and Scopus \parencite{culbert2025reference}. These limitations frame the corpus as a controlled analytical sample, not as a census of global scientific production.

\section{Corpus Scope, Eligibility Criteria, and Sampling Design}

The empirical analyses are based on a synchronized OpenAlex title--abstract corpus covering publications from 2000 to 2024. The full corpus contains 2,378,036 text-eligible works distributed across 252 subfields, 26 fields, and 4 domains. The embedding-based analysis uses a row-aligned subset of 2,344,927 embedded works across 241 analysis subfields.

Eligibility follows from the needs of semantic embedding. A retained work must contain enough text for document-level representation, and the embedding input is the concatenation of title and abstract. The extraction is restricted to English records, articles and preprints, valid metadata, and minimum title and abstract lengths.

\input{tables/tab_03_corpus_filters}

These filters improve comparability but impose clear boundaries on interpretation. English-only records reduce linguistic heterogeneity while introducing language bias. The article and preprint restriction excludes document types with different communicative functions. The title--abstract requirement improves semantic consistency but can underrepresent older records and fields with weaker abstract coverage.

The corpus uses a balanced annual sampling design. For subfield \(s\) and year \(t\), the planned annual sample size is

\[
n_{s,t}^{planned} = \min \left( N_{s,t}^{eligible}, 400 \right),
\]

where \(N_{s,t}^{eligible}\) is the number of works satisfying the eligibility filters in that subfield-year cell. Across the 25-year period, the maximum full-period target is \(400 \times 25 = 10{,}000\) works per subfield. The complete plan contains 2,431,303 planned works. The synchronized corpus retains 2,378,036 works, leaving a total shortfall of 53,267 works.

Capacity from sparse cells is not redistributed to denser cells. When a subfield-year cell contains fewer than 400 eligible works, the missing observations are recorded as shortfall. This preserves the temporal structure of the corpus and prevents large or recent fields from dominating later measures only because they produce more eligible records.

\section{Corpus Composition and Validation}

The synchronized corpus covers all four OpenAlex domains. Physical Sciences contributes the largest number of retained works, followed by Health Sciences, Social Sciences, and Life Sciences.

\input{tables/tab_03_domain_distribution}

The domain distribution reflects the OpenAlex taxonomy, the annual cap, and the availability of eligible title--abstract records. It should not be read as the true distribution of global scientific output. The sampling design deliberately reshapes observed publication volume to support comparable semantic measurements across subfields and years.

Validation checks the planned design and the required metadata and text conditions. The synchronized validation report did not fail. Critical checks are clean: there are no duplicate work identifiers, no rows outside 2000--2024, no rows in 2025 or 2026, no missing subfield, field, domain, or primary-topic identifiers, no missing title or abstract fields, no records below the minimum text thresholds, no non-English rows, and no disallowed work types.

The remaining limitation is coverage rather than validity. Out of 6,300 diagnostic subfield-year rows, 5,493 reach the annual target of 400 works and 807 remain below target. These below-target cells account for the total shortfall of 53,267 works. Most shortfalls arise because the corresponding subfield-year cells contain fewer than 400 eligible works, or because download and filtering could not fully recover the planned count.

\section{From Validated Corpus to Analysis Subset}

Corpus construction proceeds through a reproducible sequence: taxonomy definition, annual count estimation, corpus planning, sample construction, work retrieval, eligibility filtering, and validation. The resulting title--abstract corpus is the textual basis for the embedding stage. Implementation-level artifact names and execution commands are reserved for Appendix A.

The embedding stage converts this validated corpus into a row-aligned analysis matrix. The analysis subset is smaller than the full corpus because metric computation requires valid embeddings, unambiguous metadata alignment, and sufficient observations per analytical unit. This subset contains 2,344,927 embedded works across 241 analysis subfields.

The distinction between the full corpus and the analysis subset is therefore methodological. The full corpus describes the validated text extraction; the analysis subset defines the observations that enter the embedding-space morphology metrics.

\section{Implications for Empirical Interpretation}

The corpus supports comparative measurement of semantic morphology, not claims about total scientific production, research funding, institutional capacity, citation impact, or causal scientific development. Its design serves three purposes: cross-disciplinary comparability through annual caps, temporal comparability through a stable 2000--2024 sampling structure, and textual consistency through title--abstract eligibility.

The later results should be interpreted as measurements of a balanced OpenAlex title--abstract corpus represented in a shared embedding space. They are reproducible indicators of how retained scientific works are distributed under a specific data source, taxonomy, sampling design, and text representation. They are not objective maps of science itself.
