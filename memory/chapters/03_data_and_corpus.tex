\section{Why OpenAlex Subfields?}

OpenAlex is the primary bibliographic data source for the thesis: an open index of scholarly works, authors, venues, institutions, and research concepts designed for large-scale reproducible analysis \parencite{priem2022openalex}. It is used because the thesis needs a corpus that can be queried at scale, linked to a documented field hierarchy, filtered through explicit metadata, and reconstructed without proprietary infrastructure. Its openness is therefore not only convenient; it is part of the research design, because the corpus can be documented, audited, and rebuilt from the same public source.

The main analytical unit is the OpenAlex \textit{subfield}. Subfields are granular enough to distinguish research areas, large enough to support distributional measurement, and stable enough for comparisons over time. They are aggregated into \textit{fields} and \textit{domains} when broader summaries are required. Each work is assigned to a subfield through its primary-topic subfield, preserving a one-work--one-subfield structure for the main analysis.

OpenAlex subfields are used as practical units of comparison, not as natural boundaries of scientific knowledge. Coverage differences, metadata incompleteness, classification uncertainty, and language bias remain part of the corpus being studied. Comparative evidence shows that open databases can support broad science mapping while varying by database, discipline, and document type \parencite{martinmartin2021google}; recent work also documents OpenAlex metadata limits, including abstract coverage, despite broadly comparable reference coverage under cleaned comparisons with Web of Science and Scopus \parencite{culbert2025reference}. The corpus is therefore a controlled analytical sample, not a census of global scientific production.

\section{Why Title and Abstract?}

The empirical analyses use a synchronized OpenAlex title--abstract corpus covering publications from 2000 to 2024. The full corpus contains 2,378,036 text-eligible works across 252 subfields, 26 fields, and 4 domains. The embedding-based analysis uses a row-aligned subset of 2,344,927 embedded works across 241 analysis subfields.

Full text would contain more information, but it is not consistently available at this scale and would introduce strong availability bias across publishers, fields, and years. Bibliographic metadata alone would be too limited for semantic morphology. Title--abstract records are a practical middle ground: they are available for a large corpus, comparable across fields, and rich enough to represent the central problem, method, and contribution of a paper.

Eligibility follows from this design. A retained work must contain enough title--abstract text for document-level representation. The extraction is restricted to English records, articles and preprints, valid metadata, and minimum title and abstract lengths.

\section{Why a Balanced Corpus?}

The corpus is balanced because the thesis measures shape, not publication volume. If raw publication volume dominated the analysis, large and text-rich fields could look broader simply because they contribute more papers. The annual cap reduces this risk and makes comparisons more about morphology than size.

For subfield \(s\) and year \(t\), the planned annual sample size is

\[
n_{s,t}^{planned} = \min \left( N_{s,t}^{eligible}, 400 \right),
\]

where \(N_{s,t}^{eligible}\) is the number of works satisfying the eligibility filters in that subfield-year cell. Across the 25-year period, the maximum full-period target is \(400 \times 25 = 10{,}000\) works per subfield. The complete plan contains 2,431,303 planned works. The synchronized corpus retains 2,378,036 works, leaving a total shortfall of 53,267 works.

Capacity from sparse cells is not redistributed to denser cells. When a subfield-year cell contains fewer than 400 eligible works, the missing observations are recorded as shortfall. This preserves temporal structure and prevents large or recent fields from dominating later measures only because they produce more eligible records.

\section{Validation and Coverage}

The synchronized corpus covers all four OpenAlex domains. Physical Sciences contributes the largest number of retained works, followed by Health Sciences, Social Sciences, and Life Sciences.

\input{tables/tab_03_domain_distribution}

The domain distribution reflects the OpenAlex taxonomy, the annual cap, and eligible title--abstract availability. It should not be read as the true distribution of global scientific output. The sampling design deliberately reshapes observed publication volume to support comparable semantic measurements across subfields and years.

Validation checks the planned design and the required metadata and text conditions. The critical checks passed: no duplicate work identifiers, no rows outside 2000--2024, no rows in 2025 or 2026, no missing subfield, field, domain, or primary-topic identifiers, no missing title or abstract fields, no records below the minimum text thresholds, no non-English rows, and no disallowed work types.

The remaining limitation is coverage rather than validity. Out of 6,300 checked subfield-year cells, 5,493 reach the annual target of 400 works and 807 remain below target. These below-target cells account for the total shortfall of 53,267 works.

\section{From Corpus to Analysis}

Corpus construction proceeds through taxonomy definition, annual count estimation, corpus planning, sample construction, work retrieval, eligibility filtering, and validation. The resulting title--abstract corpus is the textual basis for the embedding stage. Appendix A records artifact names and execution commands.

The embedding stage converts the validated corpus into a row-aligned analysis matrix. The analysis subset is smaller than the full corpus because metric computation requires valid embeddings, unambiguous metadata alignment, and sufficient observations per analytical unit. It contains 2,344,927 embedded works across 241 analysis subfields.

\paragraph{What this establishes.}
The thesis does not compare raw publication volume. It compares balanced distributions of retained title--abstract records. This makes the later morphology metrics more comparable across fields and years, while keeping the corpus explicit: a controlled OpenAlex sample represented through scientific document embeddings.
