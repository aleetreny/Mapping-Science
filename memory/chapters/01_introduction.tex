\section{The Problem}

Scientific fields are usually named before they are measured. A label tells us that a paper belongs to a field, a topic model tells us something about its themes, a citation network tells us how it is linked to other work, and a map gives it a position on a page. These views are useful, but none of them directly tells us how the papers of a field are arranged.

Two fields can belong to different domains but have similarly shaped paper distributions. Two fields can be close in semantic location but differ in dispersion, local density, hubness, or temporal movement. A centroid can say where a field is, but it cannot say whether the field is compact, broad, locally dense, fragmented, hub-dominated, or changing through time.

This thesis starts from that gap. It treats each scientific subfield as a distribution of papers in a shared scientific document embedding space and asks what becomes visible when the shape of that distribution is measured directly.

The central claim is simple: scientific fields have measurable morphology. They have spread, local neighborhoods, hubs, directions of variation, and movement over time. These dimensions do not replace taxonomies, citation maps, topic models, or visual maps. They add a different layer of measurement: not only what a field is called or where it is located, but how its papers occupy semantic space.

\section{The Alternative: Fields as Embedded Paper Distributions}

The thesis represents OpenAlex title--abstract records with SPECTER2 document embeddings. Each retained paper receives a 768-dimensional vector, and each subfield becomes a cloud of papers in the same representation space. Once fields are represented this way, several direct questions become possible.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item How broadly do the papers of a field spread around their center?
    \item How close are papers to their nearest neighbors?
    \item Do some papers become local hubs for many others?
    \item Is variation concentrated in a few directions or spread across many?
    \item How does the field move or change between 2000 and 2024?
    \item Which fields have similar morphology even when they belong to different taxonomic domains?
\end{enumerate}

These are not questions about publication volume, citation impact, or scientific quality. They are questions about arrangement. A field can be broad but locally regular. It can be compact but hub-concentrated. It can be taxonomically distant from another field while having a similar morphology profile.

\section{Research Questions}

The objective is to develop and apply a reproducible framework for measuring field morphology with OpenAlex metadata and SPECTER2 document embeddings \parencite{priem2022openalex,singh2023scirepeval}. The analysis is conditional on a transparent corpus, a defined taxonomy, a specific representation model, and metrics computed in the original embedding space.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item How can scientific subfields be represented as distributions of papers in a shared scientific embedding space?
    \item Which interpretable metrics summarize the shape and movement of those distributions?
    \item How do scientific domains, fields, and subfields differ in static morphology?
    \item How does morphology change across the 2000--2024 period?
    \item Does morphological similarity follow OpenAlex taxonomy, or does it reveal additional structure?
    \item Why should clustering be read as descriptive compression rather than as a new taxonomy of science?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Contribution and Structure}

The thesis makes five contributions. First, it constructs a balanced OpenAlex title--abstract corpus covering 2000--2024, with documented links between works, years, subfields, fields, and domains. Second, it builds a row-aligned SPECTER2 analysis matrix in which each retained work has a 768-dimensional document vector linked to validated metadata. Third, it defines an eleven-metric morphology core covering dispersion, local density, hubness, spectral structure, and temporal movement. Fourth, it applies that framework to static comparison, temporal evolution, morphological similarity, convergence, divergence, and exploratory typologies. Fifth, it keeps taxonomy, semantic location, static shape, temporal movement, and visualization separate.

That separation is the main intellectual point. A domain label captures one kind of organization. A centroid captures another. A visual map communicates selected relationships. Morphology asks a different question: how are the papers of a field arranged?

Chapters 2 to 5 define the basis of the framework: science mapping, corpus construction, SPECTER2 representation, and the metric core. Chapters 6 to 9 apply the framework to static shape, temporal movement, morphological similarity, convergence, divergence, and typological compression. Chapters 10 and 11 return to the larger argument: no single map is enough, because a field has a name, a location, a shape, a trajectory, and relations to other fields.
